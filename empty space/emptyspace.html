The exploration of empty space, the enigma of dark matter, and the behavior of light in a vacuum have long been focal points of inquiry within the realm of physics. However, despite extensive study, true 'empty space' remains elusive. Nearly a century ago, in 1928, the brilliant mathematician Paul Dirac stumbled upon a remarkable revelation while toying with equations: Empty space is far from empty. It pulsates with ceaseless activityâ€”an incessant dance of matter and antimatter,creating, destroying and recreatingh, too rapid for human perception to capture.
This insight brought Shivani's mind to the Upanishads, an ancient Indian text dating back five millennia. They convey a concept embodied by the Sanskrit term 'Kha,' loosely translated as 'nothing' in English. However, a more apt interpretation might be 'the unmanifest and unseen potential with boundless possibilities.' To grasp the essence of Kha, envision the empty space within the hub of a wheel, the void where an axle resides. When this axle turns, it propels the wheel forward.

Could the path to infinity truly lie in realising that what we do not see with our senses may in reality be the source of infinite possibilities?
