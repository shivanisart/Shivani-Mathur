The pathway from Lucknow to London

Originally from India, Shivani Mathur came to London in June 1998 on an allowance of 35 pounds per day, expecting to spend two weeks here. Financial markets were in turmoil, her education background seemed 'just right' and she found opportunity in the all male-world of investment banking. Although her profile was unlike that of a typical investment banker, she took the chance.

She soon rose to be recognised as the top two percent of women in the financial sector in London, but something in her was restless to look beyond material dreams. As an outsider, her vision was of a world where extraordinary opportunities are accessible to ordinary people with big dreams, grit, humility, an incessant quest for learning. People who do not shy from taking alternate pathways.

How Art entered her life

Shivani realized that art has the power to empower, provoke thought, and bring about positive change. Thus, alongside her corporate career, she embarked on a parallel artistic journey, igniting conversations around disruption, opportunity, social justice, and sustainability. Her dedication to both finance and art converged when she enrolled in a Master's degree in Art and Science at Central Saint Martins during the pandemic. This transformative experience earned her a distinction and an award for sustainability by the prestigious 'Maison Zero' - a joint award by LVMH and the University of the Arts, London. Her research paper titled "Pregnant with infinity" was peer reviewed and published by Leonardo Labs. These days, in her studio, she immerses herself in clay, paint, and discarded materials like broken mirrors and eggshells. Each work explores subtle cracks symbolizing life's disruptions. These cracks evolve unpredictably over time and begin to reflect, transmit, and amplify light. Her artwork celebrates cracks and imperfections as pathways of light. Light awakens the dormant potential within each and every one of us. Potential that can empower us to see the invisible, touch the intangible, and achieve the seemingly impossible. I explore a wide variety of media, from discarded materials like glass, mirrors, and eggshells to classic forms like oil paint and modern techniques such as generative art.

Inspiration

Her current work is directly influenced by research in three distinct disciplines

- Ancient Indian philosophy from the Upanishads, emphasizing self-discovery and interconnectedness, encouraging individuals to look within themselves to understand the universe.
- The Japanese art of Kintsugi, celebrating imperfections and breakage.
- Quantum physics, which explores subatomic particle behavior and questions how they are related to the cosmic scale.

At the core of her creative practice lies the belief that art transforms lives.
